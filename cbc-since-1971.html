The Congressional Black Caucus · 1971 – Now

Whose name is
on the law?

The CBC was founded in 1971 with 13 members. In the half-century since, Black legislators have written real, structural law — and they've been handed holidays, museums, and bills that crossed the finish line under someone else's name. This sorts fifty years into what actually moved, and what only looked like it did. Every claim is sourced.

01

Three categories

Not all wins are equal. A structural law that moves money or rights is a different thing from a holiday — and both are different from a bill you carried for years that passed with another name on it.

Landmark law — with teeth

Moved money, power, or rights. Carried a Black author's name or hand, and became enforceable law.

Parren Mitchell — 10% minority-business set-aside (1977) · Ron Dellums — Anti-Apartheid Act, over Reagan's veto (1986) · William Clay Sr. — Family & Medical Leave Act (1993) · Bobby Scott — Fair Sentencing Act (2010)

Naming & recognition

Real, hard-won, symbolically powerful — but a holiday is not a housing program.

MLK Day (1983) · Nat'l Museum of African American History & Culture — John Lewis, reintroduced 15 years running (2003) · Juneteenth (2021)

Carried for years — someone else's name

Championed for years or decades by a Black member, then enacted under a white legislator's name — or stripped of the part that did the work.

Juneteenth — Jackson Lee's H.R.1320 carried for years; enacted as Markey's S.475 · Humphrey-Hawkins — Hawkins wrote it; his name went second, and the job guarantee was cut

The one everybody gets wrong

MLK Day is not a stolen-credit story.

Conyers introduced an MLK holiday bill every year from 1968 — but the bill that became law in 1983 was sponsored by Katie Hall, the first Black congresswoman from Indiana. A Black woman's name is on the King holiday. Use it as the flex, never as the grievance — the correction is what makes the rest of the argument bulletproof.

02

The timeline

Year by year, color-coded by category. What passed, what was named, what was stripped, and what never got a vote at all.

Landmark law Naming Stripped / vetoed / never passed Milestone
1971 Founded

Congressional Black Caucus founded

13 members · Charles Diggs, chair

Chisholm, Clay, Conyers, Dellums, Hawkins, Mitchell, Rangel, Stokes and more. The institution the rest of this is built on.

1975 Law · teeth

Voting Rights Act — extended to language minorities

Barbara Jordan (D–TX)

Pushed VRA protections to Mexican Americans and other language-minority groups and brought Texas under federal coverage. A structural expansion, not a symbol.

1977 Law · teeth

Minority business 10% set-aside

Parren Mitchell (D–MD) · the "Mitchell Amendment"

Required 10% of federal public-works funds go to minority-owned firms. Upheld by the Supreme Court in Fullilove v. Klutznick (1980). Public Works Employment Act of 1977

1978 Stripped · co-billed

Humphrey-Hawkins Full Employment Act

Augustus Hawkins (D–CA), founding CBC member

Hawkins authored it — but the marquee reads Humphrey first, and the government job-guarantee at its heart was stripped before passage. He later said the law was being ignored. Pub. L. 95-523

1983 Naming

Martin Luther King Jr. federal holiday

Katie Hall (D–IN) sponsored · Conyers championed since 1968

The enacted bill (H.R. 3706) was Hall's — a Black congresswoman. The credit here landed where it belonged. Pub. L. 98-114

1986 Law · over veto

Comprehensive Anti-Apartheid Act

Ron Dellums (D–CA)

Initiated the fight in 1972; passed 14 years later over Reagan's veto — the first override of a presidential foreign-policy veto in the 20th century. Pub. L. 99-440

1993 Law · teeth

Family and Medical Leave Act

William L. Clay Sr. (D–MO), founding CBC member

Job-protected leave when a child is born. Note the ceiling: it's unpaid, and the U.S. still has no federal paid leave. H.R. 1, 103rd Congress

1993 Law · teeth

National Voter Registration Act ("Motor Voter")

John Conyers (D–MI), chief House sponsor

Tied voter registration to the DMV and public-assistance offices — one of the largest franchise expansions since the VRA.

1994 Contested

Violent Crime Control & Law Enforcement Act

The CBC split — see below

Two-thirds of Black House members voted yes. The Racial Justice Act — the CBC's price for support — was stripped to win Senate votes. The turning point of the carceral story.

2003 Naming

National Museum of African American History & Culture

John Lewis (D–GA)

Reintroduced it every session for 15 years against filibuster threats. Museum opened 2016. Pub. L. 108-184

2010 Law · partial

Fair Sentencing Act

Bobby Scott (D–VA), lead House champion

Cut the crack-vs-powder cocaine sentencing disparity from 100:1 to 18:1 — reduced, not eliminated, and not retroactive until 2018. Pub. L. 111-220

2018 Law

First Step Act

Hakeem Jeffries (D–NY) & Cory Booker (D–NJ)

Sentencing and reentry reform; made parts of the Fair Sentencing Act retroactive. "First Step" was the framing — limited by design. Pub. L. 115-391

2021 Naming white-name finish

Juneteenth National Independence Day

Sheila Jackson Lee (D–TX) championed · enacted as Markey's S.475

Jackson Lee carried H.R. 1320 for years; the vehicle that became law was the Senate bill. Barbara-Rose Collins introduced the first federal Juneteenth bill in the 1990s. Pub. L. 117-17

2020–21 Died in Senate

George Floyd Justice in Policing Act

Karen Bass (D–CA)

Passed the House twice. Never became law — Bass–Scott negotiations collapsed in 2021. Maximal performance, zero structural change.

1989 – now Never a floor vote

H.R. 40 — Commission to Study Reparation Proposals

John Conyers → Sheila Jackson Lee → Cori Bush

Introduced every Congress since 1989. Has never received a floor vote in either chamber. The bill number itself references "40 acres and a mule."

04

The 1994 flip

When the bill is a holiday, watch how the credit drifts. When the bill builds prisons, the record is just as clear.

2⁄3of Black House members voted YES

On final passage of the 1994 crime bill, 24 of the 36 voting Black House members voted for it (House Roll Call 416, 235–195). Chairman Kweisi Mfume: "we have put our stamp on this bill." John Lewis, Maxine Waters, John Conyers, and Charles Rangel voted no.

The catch that matters for the carceral thread: the Racial Justice Act — the CBC's demand to let defendants challenge a death sentence with statistical evidence of racial bias — was dropped to win Senate votes. The bloc that supplied the margin got its one structural demand stripped, and many voted yes anyway.
