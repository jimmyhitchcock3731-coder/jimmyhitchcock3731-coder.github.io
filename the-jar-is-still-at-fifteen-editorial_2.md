# The Jar Is Still At Fifteen
### If you sent me to that floor, here's the truth I'd tell — starting with my own side of the aisle

*By Jimmy Hitchcock · P.O.A.S.T.*

---

Start with the number, because the number is the whole argument.

For every hundred dollars a white family holds in wealth, a Black family holds about fifteen. That's not a slogan. That's the Federal Reserve. And here is the part that should stop you cold: that number has barely moved in more than thirty years. Not through Democratic administrations, not through Republican ones, not through the largest, most credentialed, most photographed generation of Black elected officials this country has ever produced.

We had the seats. We had the speeches. We had the holidays and the museums and the moments. And the jar is still at fifteen.

So put me in the seat. Imagine you sent me to that floor. I'd take the oath, and then I'd turn around and tell you the truth even the people who look like me don't want said out loud: **representation is not power.** A face that looks like yours in the room is not the same as a hand that fills your jar. We were taught to treat them as the same thing, and that lie has cost us fifty years.

## The brutal part

The Congressional Black Caucus was founded in 1971 with thirteen members and a beautiful idea. And its members wrote real law — I've documented it, every bill and public-law number: the anti-apartheid sanctions Ron Dellums forced through over Reagan's veto, the family leave William Clay authored, motor-voter, the museum John Lewis fought fifteen years to build. I will not spit on that. It was real.

But line the record up and look at it honestly. When it was time for a *holiday*, the machine moved. When it was time to *name* a building, the machine moved. And when it was time to do the one thing that fills the jar — put wealth back in the hands of the people it was stolen from — the machine found sixty reasons to move slow, water it down, or hand the credit to somebody else.

Reparations? There is a bill — H.R. 40 — that asks only to *study* the question. Just study it. John Conyers introduced it every single Congress from 1989 until he left. Sheila Jackson Lee carried it after him. It has never once received a floor vote. Thirty-five years, and we could not get a *conversation*.

And here is the wound underneath the wound. Some of the people we sent to fill the jar started to love the room more than the jar — because the cameras are in that room, the soft chair is in that room, and the corporate checks come *in* that room.

I'm going to name it, because you asked me to, and because the receipts are public.

The Caucus tells you it wants to ban private prisons. It says so on its own website. And the public record shows the private-prison company CoreCivic cut two twenty-five-thousand-dollar checks to the Caucus's own Institute — one in 2018, one in 2019 — with three members named as the honorees: **Bennie Thompson, James Clyburn, and Cedric Richmond.** GEO Group — the other prison giant — put ten thousand dollars into the Caucus Foundation in 2017. **Sanford Bishop** personally took twenty-one thousand dollars from these companies — as much as any member of the entire House. The Caucus's own PAC swore this money off back in 2016 after activists shamed them into it. The Institute just kept cashing the checks anyway.

You cannot take the incarcerators' money and be trusted to empty their cages. That is not an accusation. That is arithmetic.

And it did not start with the money — it started with the votes. In 1994, when the crime bill that helped build the modern prison state came to that floor, two-thirds of the Black members voted yes. **Clyburn** voted yes. **Bishop** voted yes. **Thompson** voted yes. Chairman **Kweisi Mfume** said the Caucus had "put its stamp on this bill." The one demand that would have protected us — the Racial Justice Act, letting a defendant challenge a death sentence with proof of racial bias — was stripped out to win Senate votes, and most of them voted yes anyway.

Some said no. **John Lewis** said no. **Maxine Waters** said no. **John Conyers** said no. **Charles Rangel** said no. I say their names too, because they are the proof that it was always a *choice* — that being outnumbered was never the whole story. Some chose the jar. Some chose the room.

That is the brutal truth: **we were captured.** Not all of us. But enough. The movement that filled those seats went quiet, and the interests that fund those seats got loud, and the gap between the two is exactly the size of the gap in the jar.

And even the wins get their name changed at the register. Augustus Hawkins *wrote* the full-employment bill; we call it Humphrey-Hawkins, the white senator's name first, and the job guarantee — the whole point — was cut before it passed. Sheila Jackson Lee carried Juneteenth for years; the version that actually became law was Senator Ed Markey's. We do the work. Somebody else gets the marquee.

## What I'd do with the seat

So here is how I'd carry it, and I'd say it out loud on day one so you could hold me to it.

One test for every vote: **does this put marbles in the jar?** Not does it look good. Not does it get me on the panel. Does it move wealth, or freedom, or the vote? If it doesn't, let somebody else cut the ribbon.

I'd go where the wealth is actually written — the tax-writing committee — because marbles move through the tax code and the mortgage, not the press conference. I'd fight for the thing that fills the *empty* jar on purpose: seeded accounts for every child, weighted so the kids with the least get the most, so an eighteen-year-old walks into adulthood owning something instead of owing everything. And I'd stop wasting our hope on heroic bills designed to die at sixty votes, when the money bills pass at fifty-one through reconciliation. The play isn't a beautiful speech and a funeral. The play is muscling real wealth into the one vehicle that only needs a simple majority — and refusing to leave without it.

And here is the line I'd carve into the door: **I would not take their money.** Not from the prison contractors. Not from the payday lenders. Not from the corporate lobbies that have bought quiet from better people than me. The day the soft chair matters more than the jar, I've already lost — I've just started pretending I haven't. So I'd keep the seat disposable. A leader who can't afford to lose the seat can't afford to use it.

I'd also tell you the math straight. We are thirteen out of a hundred. Thirteen hands cannot beat fifty-one. Which means we win only when we stop selling this as a favor to Black people and start telling the truth — that a family in the coalfields and a family on the block need the *same* down payment, the same shot, the same jar filled. That's not watering down the mission. It's the only version of the mission that has ever passed.

## Now the warm part — because this is where we actually win

But I need to tell you something, and I mean it as good news.

The seat was never going to save us. And that is not despair. That is *freedom*.

Every real thing this country was ever forced to do, it did because the people made the inside move first. Selma made the Voting Rights Act — the goodwill of Congress didn't. The lesson of fifty years isn't that our people failed us. It's that we put our whole faith in one room, and one room was never enough. The power was never supposed to live only in Washington. It was supposed to live *here* — on the block, in our hands, in what we build when we stop waiting for a savior with a gavel.

That is what P.O.A.S.T. is. That is why the jar matters to me more than the podium. We are building the thing that fills the jar from the ground up — because we *own* it, and what you own can't be lobbied away from you. A worker-owned rideshare cooperative where the drivers keep the equity instead of feeding it to an app three time zones away. Community structures that keep the money that leaves our neighborhood every single night — and let it stay, and compound, and come back around. A community loan fund built by us, for us, that answers to the block and not to a donor.

I'm going to lay all of it out for you soon — the full architecture, the numbers, the goals, in plain language you can hold in your hand and build with me. This is the promise, and the blueprint is coming.

Because here is the truth that turns brutal into beautiful: **we do not have to wait for permission to fill our own jar.** They spent fifty years teaching us that power is a seat somebody grants you. It is not. Power is a thing you build and refuse to sell.

The seat is downstream of the movement. It always was. So we build the movement so strong that whoever holds that seat can't afford to ignore us — and can't be bought out from under us.

Fifty years of faces. The jar is still at fifteen.

We're done waiting on the face. We're going to be the force.

Less noise. More movement.

The people are the power.

*— More soon. The full P.O.A.S.T. blueprint is on the way.*

### The receipts
- **Wealth gap:** Federal Reserve, Survey of Consumer Finances (2022) — roughly $15 in Black family wealth for every $100 in white family wealth; gap essentially unchanged in 30+ years.
- **H.R. 40:** Introduced every Congress since 1989; no floor vote in either chamber.
- **CoreCivic to CBC Institute:** $25,000 "honorary expenses," 2018 and again April 15, 2019; honorees Rep. Bennie Thompson (CBCI chair), Rep. James Clyburn, and Cedric Richmond (CBCI board). (American Prospect / Sludge; Prison Legal News, 2019.)
- **GEO Group to CBC Foundation:** $10,000, 2017 (tax records).
- **CBC PAC:** Stopped accepting private-prison contributions in 2016 after a Color of Change campaign; the affiliated Institute continued.
- **Sanford Bishop:** $21,000 from CoreCivic and MTC — tied for the most of any sitting member of Congress. (The Appeal, 2026.)
- **1994 crime bill:** House Roll Call 416 (Aug. 21, 1994), passed 235-195; about two-thirds of Black House members voted yes. Yes: Clyburn, Bishop, Thompson, Mfume, and others. No: Lewis, Waters, Conyers, Rangel, and others.
- **Note for fairness:** Rep. Clyburn has publicly defended his 1994 vote, citing the crack-cocaine crisis in Black communities. The donations above went largely to CBC-affiliated *institutions* (with members named as honorees) rather than being framed as personal bribes — verify each line against the primary filing before broadcast.
