Two bills · one idea · one $1,000 seed

Same seed.
One fills the jar.
One tips it to the top.

Both bills hand a newborn a $1,000 government seed. One was written so the government keeps filling the poorest child's account. The other stops at $1,000 and lets families who already have money pour in the rest. One never passed. One is law.

The one feature they removed

They kept the $1,000. They cut the part that did the work.

The whole point of a baby bond was one design choice: the government keeps depositing, and it deposits the most for the children with the least. That inverse-to-wealth scaling is what fills an empty account by age 18. Strip it out — keep only the flat $1,000 photo-op — and you're left with an account that grows fastest for the families who were already ahead. Same name. Same seed. Opposite effect.

02

The honest scorecard

The case made for Trump Accounts

  • Universal — every child qualifies, no means-testing, no stigma.
  • Builds a saving and investing habit from birth.
  • Stock-market returns can beat a 3% Treasury account over 18 years.
  • The first federal child-savings accounts ever — a precedent to build on.

What the design actually does

  • Grows with private deposits — so it rewards families with money to spare.
  • Seed-only floor is about $5,800 at 18 (the government's own number).
  • The tax-form claim adds exactly the friction that filters out the poorest.
  • Turning into a penalty-bound IRA means it isn't an at-18 down payment.

It isn't that a $1,000 head start is bad. It's that they took the one idea that could have filled the empty jar, kept the seed, and left the teeth on the cutting-room floor.
