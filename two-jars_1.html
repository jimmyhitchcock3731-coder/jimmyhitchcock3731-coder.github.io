The Two Jars

Representation is not power. A face is not a force. And the jar is still at fifteen.

Imagine a classroom with 100 kids. To change any rule, you need 51 hands up. That's the deal.

About 13 of those kids are a special group. Call them the Helpers. Their job is to stand up for the kids who've been treated unfairly for a long, long time.

For 50 years, the Helpers have stood up every single day. Speeches. Hands up. Real work. That part is true — they are not lazy and they are not fake.

But here's what even a five-year-old can see. On the teacher's desk there are two jars of marbles. One jar — the kids who were always treated fair — has 100 marbles. The other jar — the kids the Helpers stand up for — has 15.

For 50 years the Helpers gave speeches about those jars. They got a holiday named. They got a museum built. They even won a few real rules — good ones, like "you can stay home when your baby is born."

Then go look at the jar. It still has about 15 marbles.

That's the whole secret. The speeches were loud. The jar didn't fill.

Why didn't it fill?

One — they were always outnumbered. 13 hands can't beat 51. To fill the jar, the Helpers had to ask the other kids — the same kids whose jar was already full. On the big stuff, those kids almost always said no. So the Helpers learned to ask for things they could win: a holiday, a museum, a day off. Easier to get. Doesn't fill the jar.

Two — some Helpers started liking the front of the room. Up front you get the cameras and the nice chair. After a while, a few of them cared more about keeping the nice chair than filling the jar. Not all of them. But enough to notice.

The biggest thing that never happened

One kid stood up every single year for 35 years and said: "Can we PLEASE just talk about why one jar has 100 and the other has 15?" Just talk. Thirty-five years. The answer was always "not today." They never even had the conversation.

The jar you can't see

There's one more jar. The timeout chair. The kids the Helpers stand up for get sent to timeout about 5 times more often. And 30 years ago, a bunch of the Helpers voted to build more timeout chairs, because they thought it would keep the class safe. Some said no. Most said yes. The chairs got built. They're still full.

Having somebody who looks like you stand up front is not the same as filling your jar. That's the difference between a face and a force.

For 50 years we got a lot of faces. The jar is still at 15. That's not nothing. But don't let anybody tell you it's a lot.
